**Jurisprudential Architecture of Legal Intake: A Comprehensive Framework for Multi-Disciplinary Client Onboarding and Risk Management in the United States**

The inception of the attorney-client relationship within the United States legal system is governed by a sophisticated diagnostic process known as intake. This procedure transcends mere administrative data collection, functioning instead as a critical evidentiary and ethical screening mechanism that determines the viability of litigation, the presence of non-waivable conflicts, and the preservation of claims through statutory compliance. Professional legal intake requires a granular inquiry into the client's history, the adverse party's identity, and the specific temporal and jurisdictional variables that dictate the choice of law and the application of procedural bars. The following report delineates the exhaustive categories of questions necessitated across diverse practice areas, weaving the substantive legal requirements with the procedural imperatives of modern law firm management.

**The Ethical Sentinel: Conflict of Interest and Professional Clearance**

The primary objective of legal intake is the avoidance of professional negligence and ethical violations through a robust conflict check. Under the Model Rules of Professional Conduct, specifically Rules 1.7, 1.8, and 1.9, a lawyer is prohibited from representing a client if the representation involves a concurrent conflict of interest, where the interests of one client are directly adverse to another, or where there is a significant risk that the representation will be materially limited by the lawyer's responsibilities to a third party or the lawyer's own personal interests.

**Name and Entity Variations in Conflict Resolution**

To ensure the integrity of the conflict check, intake attorneys must collect comprehensive name data for all parties. This includes not only the potential client but also any related individuals or entities whose involvement could trigger an imputed disqualification across an entire firm. The failure to identify a subsidiary of an adverse corporate client, or a former name of a spouse in a domestic matter, can lead to catastrophic disqualification late in the litigation lifecycle.

| Subject Category | Data Points Required for Conflict Screening | Legal Implication of Inquiry |
| :--------------- | :---------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Potential Client | Full legal name, maiden names, aliases, nicknames, and former marital names. | Identification of prior representation or adverse interests in unrelated matters. |
| Adverse Parties  | Primary defendants, co-defendants, third-party claimants, and known insurance carriers. | Detection of direct adversity or "material limitation" conflicts under Rule 1.7. |
| Corporate Affiliates | Parent companies, subsidiaries, trade names (d/b/a), and major shareholders/partners. | Prevention of "enterprise-level" conflicts where the firm represents a related corporate entity. |
| Legal Counsel    | Names of currently or previously involved attorneys for the opposing party. | Identification of "lawyer-side" conflicts or prior associations with the firm. |

Beyond party names, intake must investigate the "personal interest" conflicts of the attorney. This includes inquiries into whether any attorney at the firm is seeking employment with an entity adverse to the potential client, which may reasonably affect the lawyer's professional judgment. In matters involving former government employees, the intake protocol must determine if the lawyer participated personally and substantially in the matter during their government tenure, as this necessitates strict screening and notice procedures under Rule 1.11.

**The Temporal Barrier: Jurisdictional and Statutory Analysis**

The determination of the statute of limitations is perhaps the most urgent component of the intake process. The "lex fori," or the law of the place where the action is brought, typically governs the limitation period, though complex "conflict of laws" questions can arise when an incident occurs in one state but is litigated in another. Intake lawyers must perform a "procedural vs. substantive" analysis to determine if a foreign jurisdiction's statute applies to the legal relation in question.

**Foundational Timeline Inquiries**

To safeguard the client's rights, the intake must establish a definitive timeline. The inquiry starts with the "trigger date"—the moment the breach or injury occurred—but must also explore the "discovery rule," which may toll the statute of limitations if the client could not have reasonably known of the harm until a later date.

1.  **Date and Location of Incident**: Where did the harm occur? This determines the potentially applicable state law and the relevant statute of limitations.
2.  **Date of Discovery**: When did you first learn of the possible negligence or injury? In medical malpractice or latent toxic torts, this date is legally distinct from the date of the act.
3.  **Tolling Events**: Have there been any periods of incapacity, or was the client a minor at the time? These factors can extend the time to file.
4.  **Administrative Deadlines**: In employment or government tort cases, are there shorter "notice of claim" requirements that must be met before filing a lawsuit?.

**Tort and Casualty Litigation: Personal Injury and Medical Malpractice**

In the realm of tort law, the intake process is designed to evaluate the four pillars of negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages. The intake attorney acts as a preliminary investigator, identifying not only the strengths of the claim but also the "warts" that might affect valuation, such as comparative fault or pre-existing medical conditions.

**Liability and Comparative Negligence**

The initial interview must drill down into the mechanics of the injury. For a motor vehicle accident, the inquiry is straightforward (e.g., "Did the defendant run a red light?"), but in medical malpractice or product liability, the inquiry is significantly more complex, requiring an assessment of whether the provider deviated from the standard of care.

*   **The Narrative of Fault**: Who do you think did something wrong and why? This question forces the client to articulate the theory of liability, which the lawyer must then map onto the legal standard.
*   **Physical Evidence and Documentation**: Are there photographs of the scene or the injuries? Was a police report filed? Do you have the name and badge number of the investigating officer?.
*   **Witness Identification**: Were there any bystanders? Do you have their contact information? The credibility and availability of independent witnesses are primary drivers of case value.

**Damages, Valuation, and Medical History**

A lawyer must determine the "value" of the case to decide if it is economically feasible to pursue. This requires a comprehensive review of both economic and non-economic damages. Intake must identify the "collateral source" of any payments already received, such as health insurance or workers' compensation, as these may be subject to subrogation liens.

| Injury Assessment Category | Specific Intake Questions                            | Strategic Legal Relevance                                                                 |
| :------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Nature of Injury           | What specific body parts were injured? Are the symptoms current or resolved?. | Establishes the baseline for non-economic "pain and suffering" damages.                    |
| Treatment History          | List all doctors, hospitals, and therapists visited since the incident. | Documents the "medical necessity" of the treatment and creates an evidentiary trail.         |
| Pre-existing Conditions    | Have you ever injured this body part before? List all accidents in the last ten years. | Anticipates the "pre-existing condition" defense and determines the "aggravation" claim. |
| Wage and Economic Loss     | What was your income before and after the injury? Have you returned to work?. | Calculates "special damages" for lost wages and loss of future earning capacity.           |

In medical malpractice, intake must also explore the client's health prior to the routine, elective, or emergency treatment in question. If the client is calling on behalf of a deceased individual, the lawyer must investigate the cause of death listed on the death certificate, whether an autopsy was performed, and whether an estate has been opened with the probate court.

**Defensive Advocacy: Criminal Law and Constitutional Scrutiny**

Criminal defense intake is uniquely focused on the interaction between the individual and the state. The lawyer's objective is to identify constitutional violations—such as illegal searches under the Fourth Amendment or coerced statements under the Fifth Amendment —that could lead to the suppression of evidence.

**Arrest Mechanics and Constitutional Protections**

The circumstances of the arrest are often as important as the underlying charges. The lawyer must determine if the police had probable cause or a valid warrant, and if the client's rights were properly "read" during a custodial interrogation.

*   **Search and Seizure**: Did the police have a warrant? Did they ask for consent? What specific property was seized? The answers to these questions dictate whether a Motion to Suppress is viable.
*   **Miranda and Interrogation**: Were you read your rights? Did you sign any statements? Were you physically threatened or mentally harassed? Statements made without a proper waiver are subject to exclusion.
*   **Identification Procedures**: Were you asked to participate in a line-up or a "show-up"? Flawed identification procedures are a common source of wrongful convictions and provide a fertile ground for defense.

**Mitigating Factors and Background Analysis**

Criminal defense is not only about the "what" of the crime but the "who" of the defendant. Factors such as education, employment, and military service can influence bail decisions and sentencing.

*   **Mental Health and Cognitive Ability**: Do you have any history of mental health issues or PTSD? This is critical for assessing whether the client understood police commands or if they have a diminished capacity defense.
*   **Substance Abuse History**: Is there a history of drug or alcohol abuse? This information is vital for steering the client toward diversionary programs or treatment-based sentencing options.
*   **Prior Record**: Do you have any prior arrests or convictions, even if they were expunged? A defendant's "prior record score" is a primary factor in sentencing guidelines.

**Domestic Relations: Family Law and the Equitable Distribution of Lives**

Family law intake requires a sensitive yet exhaustive look into the private and financial lives of the parties. The attorney must navigate the emotional landscape while simultaneously collecting the data required for the division of marital property and the determination of child custody.

**Marital Assets and Financial Disclosures**

In jurisdictions following equitable distribution or community property models, the intake must identify all assets acquired during the marriage and distinguish them from "separate" property, such as inheritances or gifts.

| Financial Category  | Required Documentation/Questions                                                                                                       | Legal Purpose                                                            |
| :------------------ | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Tax and Income      | Federal/state returns (last 3 years), pay stubs (last 3 months), and W-2s.                                                           | Establishes the baseline for child support and alimony calculations.     |
| Real Estate         | Property deeds, current mortgage statements, and recent appraisals.                                                                  | Determines the "equity" available for division between the spouses.      |
| Retirement/ Investments | Statements for IRAs, 401(k)s, pensions, and brokerage accounts (last 12 months).                                                   | Identifies marital interests in future retirement benefits, often subject to a QDRO. |
| Debts and Liabilities | Bank loans, credit card statements, and pending tax liabilities.                                                                     | Ensures an equitable division of marital debt along with assets.         |

The intake must also ask about any prenuptial or postnuptial agreements, as these contracts can significantly alter the default legal framework for property division.

**Child Custody and the Best Interests Standard**

When minor children are involved, the intake process shifts toward the "best interests of the child" standard. The attorney must investigate the parents' current living arrangements, the children's relationship with each parent, and any safety concerns.

*   **Custody History**: Where have the children lived for the last five years? Under the Uniform Child Custody Jurisdiction and Enforcement Act (UCCJEA), this residency history determines which state has the authority to make custody orders.
*   **Health and Well-being**: Do the children have special educational, emotional, or health needs? These factors influence the "allocation of parental responsibilities" and parenting time schedules.
*   **Safety and Domestic Violence**: Is there a history of domestic abuse or an active injunction? Screening for safety is a mandatory component of family law intake to protect the client and the children from harm.

**The Regulatory Framework: Labor and Employment Law**

Employment law intake is heavily influenced by the size of the employer and the specific statutes that govern the workplace. Federal laws such as the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act have specific employee-count thresholds that determine their applicability.

**Discrimination and Harassment Frameworks**

The core of most employment intake is identifying the "protected class" and the "adverse action." The attorney must determine if the employer's stated reason for a termination or demotion is a "pretext" for illegal discrimination.

*   **Protected Status**: Was the action based on age, race, sex, disability, religion, or pregnancy? Intake must identify the specific characteristic that triggered the employer's conduct.
*   **The Adverse Action**: Were you terminated, demoted, denied a promotion, or subjected to a hostile work environment? This establishes the "harm" required for a legal claim.
*   **Documentation and Pretext**: Did the employer provide a reason for the termination? Do you have performance reviews or internal emails that contradict that reason?.
*   **Administrative Exhaustion**: Have you filed a charge with the EEOC or a state civil rights agency? Many employment claims require a "Right to Sue" letter before a lawsuit can be filed in court.

**Wage and Hour and Workplace Injuries**

In addition to discrimination, intake must screen for wage and hour violations, such as unpaid overtime or the misclassification of employees as independent contractors.

| Employment Inquiries | Specific Questions                                                                  | Regulatory Significance                                                                     |
| :------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Classification       | Does the employer control your tasks and schedule? Do you receive a W-2 or 1099?.   | Determines eligibility for workers' compensation and overtime pay under the FLSA.           |
| Hours and Pay        | Do you work "off the clock"? Are you paid for rest and meal breaks?.                | Identifies potential "wage theft" claims and statutory penalties.                           |
| Workplace Injury     | Did the injury happen while performing job duties? Was it reported to a supervisor?. | Triggers the workers' compensation "no-fault" system and protects the right to benefits.    |

**Structural Integrity: Business, Corporate, and Intellectual Property Law**

For business and corporate clients, the intake process focuses on the structural health of the entity and the protection of its most valuable assets—its intellectual property (IP). For startups, the primary risk is often the "ownership gap" where founders or contractors have not properly assigned their work to the company.

**Entity Choice and Governance**

Intake for a new business must determine the appropriate legal structure (LLC, Corporation, Partnership) based on the owners' goals regarding liability, taxation, and fundraising.

*   **Ownership and Management**: Who are the initial investors? Who has the authority to sign contracts or open bank accounts? Clear governance documents are the first line of defense against "founder disputes".
*   **Business Purpose and Operations**: What is the primary purpose of the business? In which states will it operate? This determines where the company must register to "do business".

**Intellectual Property (IP) Protection and Vesting**

Corporate counsel must ensure that the company actually owns the technology or brand it is marketing. This requires an exhaustive review of IP assignments.

*   **The IP Portfolio**: Does the business use trademarks (brand names/logos), patents (inventions), or trade secrets (client lists/algorithms)? Identification of these assets is the first step in protecting them.
*   **Assignment of Inventions**: Have all founders and employees signed written IP assignment agreements? Are there "work-for-hire" clauses in contractor agreements? Without these, the individual may retain ownership of the IP.
*   **Third-Party Infringement**: Are there any pending "cease and desist" letters? Have you been accused of using a competitor's trade secrets?.

**Preservation and Succession: Estate Planning and Probate**

Estate planning intake is unique because the "client" is often planning for a future in which they are no longer present or competent. The attorney must act as a fiduciary architect, identifying all assets and liabilities to ensure an orderly transfer of wealth.

**Asset Inventory and Titling**

The lawyer must understand not only what the client owns, but how it is owned. Assets that are jointly titled or have named beneficiaries (such as life insurance or 401ks) pass outside of the probate process.

*   **Real Estate and Personal Effects**: Where is the property located? Are there high-value items like art or jewelry that need specific bequests?.
*   **Financial and Retirement Accounts**: What is the current value of bank accounts, stocks, and bonds? Who are the current beneficiaries?.
*   **Debts and Mortgages**: What are the client's current liabilities? The estate must be liquid enough to pay these debts before heirs receive their inheritance.

**Fiduciary Designations and End-of-Life Wishes**

The client must choose the individuals who will make decisions on their behalf. Intake must explore the client's trust in these individuals and their suitability for the role.

1.  **Personal Representative (Executor)**: Who do you want to administer your estate after your death?.
2.  **Power of Attorney**: Who will handle your financial affairs if you become incapacitated?.
3.  **Guardian**: Who will care for your minor children if you are gone?.
4.  **Healthcare Directives**: Do you want a living will? Do you want to be an organ donor?.

**Social Safety Nets: Social Security Disability and Bankruptcy**

Administrative and financial distress practices require the intake attorney to verify eligibility for government-mandated relief. Both Social Security Disability (SSD) and Bankruptcy rely on strict "means-based" or "disability-based" tests.

**Social Security Disability (SSDI) and SSI**

The intake for SSD hinges on the "Blue Book" of impairments. The lawyer must prove that the client's condition is severe enough to prevent them from working for at least twelve months.

*   **Medical Evidence and Treatment**: List all doctors, hospitals, and clinics you have visited for your condition. What specific medical tests (MRI, CT, Bloodwork) have you undergone?.
*   **Vocational History**: What jobs have you held in the last 15 years? What were the physical and mental requirements of those roles?.
*   **Income and Assets**: For Supplemental Security Income (SSI), what is your current household income and the value of your bank accounts and vehicles?.

**The Bankruptcy Means Test and Asset Protection**

Bankruptcy intake is an exercise in total financial transparency. The attorney must determine if the client qualifies for Chapter 7 liquidation or Chapter 13 reorganization based on their income and expenses.

| Bankruptcy Category    | Critical Intake Inquiries                                            | Legal Objective                                                                  |
| :--------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Income Verification    | Tax returns (last 2 years) and pay stubs (last 7 months).            | Determines eligibility under the "Means Test" based on median state income.      |
| Creditor Identification | Recent bills from every creditor, including medical and payday loans. | Ensures all debt is "scheduled" and subject to the "automatic stay."             |
| Asset Transfers        | Have you sold or given away any items over $1,000 in the last year?. | Identifies potential "voidable preferences" or fraudulent transfers that could be reversed. |
| Non-Dischargeable Debt | Do you owe student loans, child support, or recent taxes?.           | Manages client expectations regarding which debts will remain after the bankruptcy. |

**The Human Element: Empathy, Credibility, and Management**

While the data collected is the "science" of intake, the "art" lies in the lawyer's assessment of the client as a person. The intake attorney must evaluate whether the potential client will be a cooperative partner or a liability during litigation.

**Credibility and Historical Reliability**

A "bad historian" can undermine a case during a deposition. Intake must assess the client's ability to stay on point and provide a clear, chronological narrative of events.

*   **Consistency**: Does the client's story change when asked the same question in a different way?
*   **Likability**: How will a jury perceive this person? Are they credible, sympathetic, or argumentative?.
*   **Cooperation**: Is the client willing to provide documentation and follow legal advice, or do they have unrealistic expectations for the outcome?.

**Managing the Attorney-Client Relationship**

Finally, the intake process must conclude with clear warnings and an explanation of the billing system. The lawyer must define the "scope of representation"—exactly what the firm will and will not do for the client.

*   **Billing and Costs**: Does the client understand the difference between a "contingency fee" and an "hourly rate"? Who is responsible for litigation costs like filing fees and expert witnesses?.
*   **Professional Warnings**: Clients must be cautioned against speaking to third parties, including insurance adjusters, or posting about their case on social media.
*   **Engagement and Non-engagement**: The process is not complete until a formal letter of engagement (or non-engagement) is sent, documenting exactly when the attorney-client relationship began.

The legal intake process is the primary gatekeeper of professional practice. By rigorously exploring the conflict-of-interest landscape, the temporal constraints of the law, and the practice-specific nuances of each claim, the intake attorney builds the foundation for successful advocacy. This comprehensive framework ensures that the firm avoids the ethical pitfalls of conflicting interests while maximizing the strategic potential of every case it chooses to accept. Consistent adherence to these protocols is the hallmark of a sophisticated and risk-aware legal practice.
